POSSESSIVE APOSTROPHE

DEFINITION

An apostrophe shows that one thing belongs to or is connected to something. This is called a possessive apostrophe.

Different uses of the apostrophe

Don't confuse it with the contraction of the verb 'to be' conjugates as 'is'

e.g

Contraction of: My mother's beautiful

Possessive apostrophe: My mother's car is beautiful

There are two cases

Case #1:

You add a (') and a (s). Even if it ends in 's'

My boss's office is big

My mother's office is big

If it is a name and it ends in 's' you can either add the (s) or no. But you always add the '

James's dog is hungry

James' dog is hungry

If the noun is composed by 2 singular nouns...

you add one 's' to the last noun, and it means that the same property belongs to all the nouns.

Stacy and Steve's car is old

you add one 's' to each noun, and it means that each noun owns a different element.

Stacy's and Steve's cars are old

Case #2: Plural Nouns

if the plural ends in 's' you only add the (')

You need your parents' permission

If the plural doesn't end in 's' you add the (') and the (s)

The children's toys are red
